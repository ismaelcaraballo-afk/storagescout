# 5-Minute Presentation Script — StorageScout: Government Policy & Incentives
## Presenting the StorageScout Tool + The Policy Story Behind the Data
### Team: Juan, Ismael, Kevin

---

## HOW THIS WORKS

StorageScout is a single-page interactive data tool your team built. It lives at:
**https://ismaelcaraballo-afk.github.io/storagescout/**

You present BY WALKING THROUGH THE SITE. The site IS the presentation.
One person talks. One person controls the laptop and scrolls. Swap roles each section.

Page structure top to bottom:
1. Hero + Stats Bar (4 big numbers)
2. Three-Act Narrative Cards (Setup / Conflict / Resolution)
3. US Battery Storage Capacity bar chart (2019-2024)
4. The Gap dual-axis chart (storage vs emissions)
5. Top States horizontal bar chart
6. State Explorer dropdown + detail chart
7. What-If Simulator slider + projection chart
8. Cost Collapse comparison bar chart

> But first — four numbers. These are at the top of the page because they frame everything.

> 26 gigawatts of battery storage installed in the US as of 2024. Sounds like a lot — we'll show you why it's not enough.

> 93% — that's how much battery costs have fallen since 2010. The technology isn't the problem anymore.

> 3.4 million megawatt-hours — that's how much clean energy California threw away last year because there wasn't enough storage to capture it.

> And 2,300 gigawatts stuck in the interconnection queue — projects approved, funded, ready to build, waiting in line to connect to the grid. That's 88 times more than what's actually installed.

> Now let's scroll down and show you WHY these numbers look like this."

**[Kevin scrolls to the Three-Act Narrative Cards]**

## SECTION 2 — THREE-ACT CARDS + COST CHART (Kevin talks, Juan on laptop — 60 seconds)

**[Screen: Setup / Conflict / Resolution cards visible. Then scroll to Cost Collapse chart.]**

> "StorageScout tells this story in three acts — Setup, Conflict, Resolution. You can read the cards, but let me walk you through the policy behind them.

> **[Juan scrolls to the Cost Collapse bar chart at the bottom]**

> This chart is the setup. Solar at $43 per megawatt-hour. Wind at $34. Battery storage at $65. Compare that to coal at $68 and natural gas at $77. Renewables plus storage are now cheaper than fossil fuels.

> But this didn't happen by accident. In August 2022, the Inflation Reduction Act — the largest climate investment in American history — put $369 billion on the table.

> The game-changer: a 30% Investment Tax Credit for standalone battery storage. Before 2022, you only got the credit if your battery was paired with solar. Now any battery project qualifies. For a $100 million grid-scale project, that's $30 million back immediately.

> On top of that — the DOE Loan Programs Office issued $40 billion in loan guarantees. The GRIP program put $10.5 billion into grid modernization. States like California and New York passed mandates requiring gigawatts of storage by specific deadlines.

> The money poured in. Now scroll up and look at what happened."

**[Juan scrolls up to the US Battery Storage Capacity bar chart]**

## SECTION 3 — CAPACITY CHART + THE GAP (Juan talks, Ismael on laptop — 60 seconds)

**[Screen: US Battery Storage Capacity bar chart (2019-2024)]**

> "Look at this chart. 2019 — 1.2 gigawatts. 2020 — 1.8. Then the IRA hits in 2022 and watch what happens. 2022 — 8.9. 2023 — 16.2. 2024 — 26 gigawatts. That's a 490% increase in three years. Policy was the ignition switch.

> But now scroll down one chart.

> **[Ismael scrolls to The Gap dual-axis chart]**

> This is the conflict. StorageScout calls it 'The Gap.' The blue line is battery storage — shooting up. The red line is power sector CO2 emissions — barely moving. 1,724 million metric tons in 2019, down to 1,546 in 2024. That's only a 10% drop while storage grew 2,067%.

> Why the gap? Two reasons.

> First — 2,300 gigawatts of projects are stuck in the interconnection queue. Average wait: 5 years. FERC only reformed the process in 2023 with Order 2023. It hasn't taken full effect.

> Second — the global race. China invested $800 billion in clean energy in 2025 — 3.5 times more than the US at $230 billion. China controls 80% of battery manufacturing. The IRA's Section 45X gives $35 per kilowatt-hour for domestic production, and 30 new factories have been announced. But factories take 3-5 years to build. We're in the gap years.

> That's what this chart shows. The growth is real. The gap is also real."

**[Ismael scrolls to Top States + State Explorer]**

> These aren't random. They're states with three things: strong sun or wind resources, state-level storage mandates, and utilities that applied for IRA tax credits early.

> California — 7,492 megawatts of storage, 105 terawatt-hours of renewable generation, but 3,400 gigawatt-hours curtailed. That means California generated clean energy and literally threw it away because the storage wasn't there yet to capture it. Enough to power 340,000 homes for a year — wasted.

> **[Kevin switches dropdown to Texas]**

> Texas — 5,200 megawatts. The most wind energy potential in the country. Also had the deadliest grid failure in modern US history — 246 people died in the 2021 freeze. Storage could have saved lives, not just money.

> The state explorer shows you that this isn't one national story. It's 50 different stories. The policy is federal but the impact is local.

> Now let me show you the most important part of StorageScout."

**[Kevin scrolls to the What-If Simulator]**

## SECTION 5 — WHAT-IF SIMULATOR (Kevin talks, Juan on laptop — 60 seconds)

**[Screen: What-If Simulator with slider at 1.0x]**

> "This is the What-If Simulator. Everything else on StorageScout shows you what IS. This shows you what COULD BE.

> Right now the slider is at 1x — current deployment rate. You can see the projection line — where storage and emissions are headed if nothing changes.

> **[Juan drags slider to 1.5x]**

> At 1.5x deployment — which is what happens if FERC clears the interconnection backlog and the new factories come online — watch the emissions projection drop. That's the gap closing.

> **[Juan drags slider to 2x]**

> At 2x — which is the DOE's target scenario — California's curtailment disappears. The wasted clean energy gets captured. Emissions drop 15% faster. And we hit 100 gigawatts by 2030.

> This is why we built StorageScout. Not to tell you storage matters — you already know that. But to let you SEE the relationship between policy, deployment, and impact. Drag the slider yourself. The data is all real. The projections are based on EIA and NREL growth models.

> The policy toolkit exists — IRA, ITC, state mandates, FERC reform, Section 45X manufacturing credits. The money is there. The technology is there. 26 gigawatts proves the model works.

> The question StorageScout answers is: what happens if we actually go faster?"

**[Leave the simulator on screen at 2x for Q&A]**

## ANTICIPATED Q&A

**Q: "Did you build this site yourselves?"**
> A: Yes. HTML, CSS, Chart.js for the interactive charts, vanilla JavaScript for the state explorer and What-If simulator. All the data is sourced from public federal datasets — EIA, NREL, CAISO, BloombergNEF.

**Q: "What happens if the IRA gets repealed?"**
> A: 60% of IRA clean energy investment flows to Republican districts — Georgia, Tennessee, Kentucky, Texas. Repealing it means killing jobs in their own districts. The economics are bipartisan even if the politics aren't.

**Q: "Why can't we just use gas plants?"**
> A: Look at the cost chart on StorageScout. Gas is $77/MWh. Battery is $65 and falling. By 2027, batteries will be cheaper than RUNNING existing gas plants — not building new ones, keeping the old ones on.

**Q: "If China controls battery manufacturing, aren't we stuck?"**
> A: Right now, yes — 80% of cells come from China. Section 45X pays $35/kWh for US-made batteries. 30 new factories announced since the IRA. But factories take 3-5 years. We're in the gap years between policy and production.

**Q: "Is 26 GW enough?"**
> A: Open the What-If simulator. Drag it to 1x. That's the current path. Now drag to 2x — that's the DOE target of 100 GW by 2030. The tool shows you the difference. 26 GW is a proof of concept, not the finish line.

**Q: "What's the most surprising thing you found?"**
> A: The curtailment number. California generated 3.4 million MWh of clean energy and threw it away. That's the equivalent of powering 340,000 homes — wasted because storage couldn't capture it. That's not a technology problem. That's a permitting problem.
